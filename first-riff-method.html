A complete guitar method · zero to player

THE
FIRST-RIFF
METHOD

You'll sound like music before you learn a single note, read a single staff, or shape a single barre chord.

Every other beginner book teaches you to be correct. This one is built around a different job entirely: keeping you from quitting in week three — the week almost everyone quits.

The problem

Guitar books don't fail at teaching.
They fail at not-getting-quit.

Pick up any classic — Hal Leonard, the Berklee Modern Method, Mel Bay — and the first weeks are note-reading, single-string melodies, and theory. You are technically progressing and emotionally flatlining. Then your fingertips hurt, nothing sounds like a song yet, and the barre chord wall appears. So you stop.

Quitting is the actual enemy. So every decision here is reverse-engineered from one question: what's the next smallest thing that will make you want to pick the guitar back up tomorrow?

How it's wired

The dopamine engine

Six rules every lesson obeys. They're the reason this works when willpower doesn't.

RULE 01

First win in five minutes

You make a real, recognizable sound before any instruction on theory, posture, or notation. The hook comes first; the homework comes after you're already in.

RULE 02

Songs first, theory smuggled

You never learn a concept "because you should." Theory appears only when it unlocks the next thing you want to play, so it always feels earned.

RULE 03

Defer every cliff

The two biggest quit-points — reading standard notation and the full barre chord — are pushed deep into the book, long after you're hooked.

RULE 04

Drill the change, not the chord

The hard part was never the shape, it's the switch between shapes. Lessons train transitions as the main event — that's why Stage 2 ships a change game.

RULE 05

Visible progress, every time

That bar pinned to the top of your screen fills as you go. The streak is the reward, and the reward is the point.

RULE 06

Name the pain, then beat it

Sore fingertips end more guitar careers than bad technique ever has. We call it out, gamify it, and get you through callus week on purpose.

Stage 0

Lesson 0 — The First Riff

⏱ 5 minutes · no theory

Before anything else, you're going to sound dangerous.

No tuning lecture, no how-to-hold-a-pick essay. Rest the guitar on your leg and let's make a riff. We're using the two thickest strings — the two closest to the ceiling. Ignore the other four. They don't exist yet.

Step 1 — find the home sound

Pluck the thickest string (the 6th) without pressing anything. That low note is home base. Hit it four times, steady. You're playing.

Step 2 — add one finger

Press the thickest string at the 3rd fret with one finger. Pluck it. A two-note vocabulary — one more than your first riff needs.

Step 3 — play the riff
E|--0---0---3---0----0--3--3---|
   open    3rd      (repeat, slow & ugly)

0 = open, no finger. One string only. Speed is the last thing to arrive, never the first.

🔥

Callus Countdown, Day 1. Your fingertip will sting — that's skin building armor, not damage. About 7–10 short days to toughen. Most quitters quit right here, in the part nobody warned them about. You've been warned: push through one week and the pain never comes back.

The payoff

That low, one-string, finger-and-open pattern is the exact engine behind the most famous beginner riffs in rock — the "everyone's first riff" sound. Day one. Five minutes. One finger.

Stage 1

Lesson 1 — The Power Chord

⏱ 10 minutes · still no theory

One shape. Move it anywhere. Instant rock.

A power chord is two fingers that make any spot on the neck sound huge — the single most reused shape in rock, punk, and metal. It dodges the hard chords because it doesn't care what key you're in.

Power Chord ("5" shape)

Index finger on the 6th string, 3rd fret.
Ring finger on the 5th string, 5th fret.

Strum just those two strings. Thick and a little mean. That's correct.

Now make it rhythmic — the palm mute

Rest the side of your strumming hand lightly on the strings near the bridge. The sound goes from ringing to a tight chug — 80% of why rock guitar sounds like rock guitar.

      chug chug   CHUG      chug chug  CHUG
E|----3----3-----3---------3----3-----3----|
A|----5----5-----5---------5----5-----5----|

Same shape throughout. The music comes from the rhythm and the mute, not from moving your fingers.

The payoff

Slide that two-finger shape to different frets and you're playing riffs. No chord name, no note, no theory — and you already sound like a band warming up.

Stage 2

Lesson 2 — Your First Change

⏱ 15 minutes · the real skill begins

Two easy chords. The hard part is the switch.

Here's the thing every book gets wrong: they drill chord shapes. But you'll learn a shape in two minutes. What actually takes time — and what makes you sound like a real player — is changing between chords cleanly. So that's what we train, directly, as a game.

Meet your first two open chords. Both are easy. Em is the single friendliest chord on the guitar.

Em
Am

Em: two fingers, 2nd fret, strum all six. Almost impossible to get wrong.

Am: shift those same two fingers down one string and add your index. Don't hit the thickest string.

The drill — and the game

The legendary practice tool is the one-minute change: pick two chords and count how many clean switches you can make in 60 seconds. Beginners start around 8–10. Within a week you'll double it. It's the most addictive thing in early guitar because the number always goes up.

⏱ The 60-Second Change Challenge

Switch Em → Am → Em → Am. Tap the big button (or press Space) every time you complete one clean change. Beat your record.

The payoff

Two chords and a clean change between them is a song engine. A huge number of real songs ride on just two chords — and you now own a repeatable way to get better at the one skill that was always the bottleneck.

Stage 3

Lesson 3 — The Magic Four

⏱ a few sessions · the big unlock

Four chords. An absurd number of songs.

There's a reason a famous comedy bit strings dozens of pop hits together over one progression: a massive share of popular music lives on the same four chords — most often G, C, D, and Em. Learn these four and their changes, and you've unlocked the radio. Tap each one to see the shape.

G major

How to actually learn four chords

Don't practice them in a circle endlessly. Practice them in pairs, using the same 60-second drill from Stage 2 — G↔C, then C↔D, then G↔Em — because the changes are the work, and you already have the tool. Anchor fingers that stay put between chords; let those be your guide.

The payoff

With G, C, D, and Em — and the four changes between them — you can strum along to more real songs than you could learn in a year. This is the stage where "I'm learning guitar" quietly becomes "I play guitar."

Stage 4

Lesson 4 — Strumming & the Pocket

⏱ ongoing · this is "feel"

Chords are the words. Rhythm is the music.

Most beginners can hold a chord but sound stiff, because nobody taught them feel. Here's the secret the books bury in notation: your strumming hand should never stop moving. It swings down-up-down-up like a pendulum the whole time — you just miss the strings on the beats you don't want to play. The motion stays constant; the contact is what changes.

This pattern — D · D U · U D U — is the most common strum in popular music. Hit play, watch it light up, and lock your hand to it. Slow it down until it's easy, then nudge it faster.

Stage 5

Lesson 5 — The Capo Cheat

⏱ 5 minutes · feels like cheating

One clamp turns your four chords into every key.

A capo is a little clamp that presses down all the strings at one fret — a movable "nut." The magic: your same G, C, D, Em shapes still work, but everything sounds higher. So when a song is in a key your open chords can't reach, you don't learn new shapes — you just move the capo. This is how a huge number of singers and players actually get through their setlists.

Stage 6

Lesson 6 — The Barre Chord (You're Ready Now)

⏱ the famous wall · approached on purpose

This is the chord that makes people quit. You won't — your hands are strong now.

Every other book hits you with the F barre chord in week two, when your hand can't take it, and that's where the guitar goes in the closet. We waited on purpose. After Stages 0–5, your fretting hand has weeks of strength behind it. Now the barre is just a technique, not a wall.

The idea: your index finger lays flat across all six strings — it becomes your capo. Then you add the E-major shape on top with your other fingers. Because nothing is "open," the whole thing is movable: slide it up the neck and it names a new chord at every fret. One shape, every chord.

The strength payoff. Barre chords are 90% hand conditioning and 10% technique. The weeks of riffs, changes, and strumming you've already done were secretly training for exactly this moment. Buzzy notes at first are normal — press near the fret, roll the finger slightly onto its edge, and it cleans up within days.

The payoff

One movable shape just handed you every major chord on the neck. Combined with the capo, the open chords, and power chords, there is now no chord in a song you can't find a way to play.

Stage 7

Lesson 7 — Your First Solo

⏱ an afternoon · the most fun in the book

One box of five notes. They all sound good together. Always.

Soloing seems like wizardry, but the entry point is almost unfair: the minor pentatonic box is a single shape of five repeating notes where — and this is the magic — you basically can't hit a wrong one. Any order, any rhythm, it sounds intentional. This is the shape behind countless guitar solos you already know.

Here it is at the 5th fret. Click the dots and actually play it. Start and end on the green "home" notes, wander between them, and you're soloing. (Turn your sound on.)

🎵 Playable Pentatonic Box

A-minor pentatonic, 5th position. Click anything. There are no wrong notes — that's the whole point.

Pro move: click a slow, simple phrase of 3–4 notes and repeat it. Repetition is what turns random notes into a lick. That's literally how solos are written.

The payoff

You just improvised. One movable box means you can solo over almost any song in that key — and because it's movable like the power chord and the barre, sliding it changes what key you're soloing in. Lead guitar, unlocked on day one of trying.

Stage 8

Lesson 8 — Make It Yours

⏱ forever · this is the fun part

You have the toolkit. Now stop taking lessons and start playing.

Everything from here is recombination. Learning a song by ear is just: find the key, try your G-C-D-Em family (capo if needed), and let your ear confirm each change — a skill you've quietly been building this whole time. Jamming with someone is the same four chords traded back and forth. And writing your own song is the easiest of all, because of a secret pop music has kept forever:

Almost any loop of your chords sounds like a song. Don't believe it? Generate one and go play it. Strum each chord for four beats, loop it, hum over the top. That's songwriting.

🎲 Song-Starter Generator

A random four-chord loop from chords you already own. Strum each for a bar, repeat, and a song appears.

Where to point your practice now

Pick real songs you love and learn them — motivation beats any exercise. Keep the 60-second change drill as a 2-minute warmup. Noodle in the pentatonic box while watching TV. And play with other humans the moment you can; nothing accelerates you faster. The structured part of your journey is over. The good part just started.

The payoff

You started unable to make a single musical sound. You can now riff, play power chords, switch between a full family of open chords in time, capo into any key, barre the whole neck, solo in a pentatonic box, and write a loop of your own. That's not "learning guitar" anymore.
